Why Office Waste Matters — Context & SDG 12

Understand the global urgency, Ireland's responsibility, and how your daily actions at work connect to the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals.

⏱ 10–12 minutes 📖 3 sections ❓ 5 quiz questions
🌱 What is SDG 12?

SDG 12 — Responsible Consumption and Production — is one of the 17 United Nations Sustainable Development Goals adopted in 2015. It means using resources more efficiently, reducing waste, and minimising environmental harm throughout a product's lifecycle.

For offices, SDG 12 means reducing unnecessary printing, managing food and packaging waste, encouraging recycling, and choosing reusable and sustainable alternatives.
♻️ Why Does Office Waste Matter?

Office waste may seem small individually — but collectively, it has significant environmental impact. Every product used in an office goes through a lifecycle. Click each impact to learn more:

💨
Carbon Emissions
Every product manufactured and shipped to your office emits CO₂. Paper alone requires intensive energy to produce, bleach, and transport.
⚡
Energy Consumption
Offices leave equipment on overnight, overheat spaces, and over-light empty rooms — all invisible drains on national energy grids.
⛏️
Resource Depletion
Paper consumes forests. Electronics use rare earth metals. Single-use plastics are derived from fossil fuels. All are finite.
🏗️
Landfill Overflow
Ireland's landfills are under severe pressure. Food, mixed waste, and incorrectly sorted recycling all end up there when not managed properly.
🏢 Why Businesses Must Act
  • Environmental compliance — EU legislation sets binding targets for businesses
  • Cost savings — Less waste means lower procurement and disposal costs
  • ESG reporting — Investors and regulators increasingly require sustainability data
  • Corporate reputation — Staff and clients increasingly choose sustainable organisations
  • Shared responsibility — Real change requires both policy and employee behaviour

The Waste Hierarchy

Not all waste management options are equal. The waste hierarchy prioritises the most sustainable approaches. Click each level to learn more.

⏱ 2–3 minutes 🔺 6 levels
🔺 The Waste Hierarchy — Click each level to expand

Best environmental outcome at the top, worst at the bottom. The higher the action, the more sustainable.

1
🚫 Prevention
Don't create waste in the first place. Share documents digitally instead of printing. Avoid unnecessary packaging. Order only what you need. Prevention saves resources, money, and energy — and is the best option by far.
BEST
2
📉 Reduce
If waste cannot be avoided entirely, reduce the amount produced. Print double-sided. Buy supplies in bulk. Reduce disposable items in the kitchen. Every reduction counts toward national targets.
GOOD
3
🔄 Reuse
Use items multiple times instead of throwing them away. Reusable coffee cups, refillable water bottles, reusing envelopes or packaging materials. Reuse keeps products in circulation longer.
GOOD
4
♻️ Recycle
Convert waste materials into new products. Paper recycling bins, plastic bottles, cardboard packaging. Recycling is useful — but it still requires energy and resources, so it's not as good as prevention or reuse.
OK
5
⚡ Recovery
Extract energy from waste through processes like waste-to-energy plants. Better than landfill, but still less sustainable than any of the options above. Should only be used when other options are exhausted.
LAST RESORT
6
🗑️ Disposal
Landfills and incineration without energy recovery. This is the final option when waste cannot be recovered or recycled. Disposal leads to resource loss, methane emissions, and long-term environmental impacts.
WORST
Key Takeaway: The higher up the hierarchy, the better the environmental outcome. Prevent → Reduce → Reuse → Recycle → Recover → Dispose. In your office, always ask: can I prevent this waste before trying to manage it?

Office Waste Streams

Even offices that appear clean and efficient generate significant waste. Explore each waste stream to identify opportunities for reduction.

📄 Paper
📦 Packaging
💻 E-Waste
🍎 Food
⚡ Energy
📄

Paper Waste

One of the largest office waste streams

📊 Typical offices use ~20 reams of paper per employee per year. Efficient offices operate with only 7 reams per person — a 65% reduction is achievable.

Common sources: printing reports, photocopying, meeting materials, draft documents.

Default printer to double-sided (duplex) printing
Share documents digitally — email, cloud, screen sharing
Use cloud storage instead of physical filing
Review documents on screen before printing
Use digital signatures — eliminate print-sign-scan cycle
📦

Packaging & Single-Use Items

Used briefly, lingers for decades

☕ A single office worker using one disposable cup per day generates 250 cups per year. For a team of 20, that's 5,000 cups annually — just from coffee.

Common sources: coffee cups, plastic bottles, snack wrappers, delivery packaging, cardboard boxes.

Bring a reusable mug, water bottle, and lunchbox
Buy office supplies in bulk to reduce packaging
Recycling cardboard delivery boxes correctly
Avoid single-use cutlery — keep reusable sets at your desk
💻

Electronic Waste (E-Waste)

Valuable, hazardous, and often overlooked

⚠️ E-waste contains copper, gold, and rare earth metals — but also hazardous substances like lead and mercury. Improper disposal contaminates soil and water supplies.

Common items: old laptops, phones, chargers, cables, keyboards, monitors, accessories.

Repair equipment before discarding it
Donate usable devices to charities or CTC Cork
Use certified e-waste recycling programmes
Never place e-waste in general waste or recycling bins
🍎

Food Waste

A hidden contributor to greenhouse gases

🌡️ Food waste decomposing in landfill produces methane — a greenhouse gas 25x more potent than CO₂ over 100 years. Office kitchens are a key source of avoidable food waste.

Common sources: office kitchens, staff lunches, company events, expired fridge items.

Take only the food you need — avoid plate waste
Label food in shared fridges with dates
Compost organic waste where facilities allow
Coordinate catering quantities for events
⚡

Energy Waste

The invisible office waste stream

🌙 A monitor left on standby overnight uses approximately 20 watts continuously — around 60 kWh per year, per monitor, doing nothing. Multiply by your office headcount.

Common sources: computers left on, lights in empty rooms, inefficient heating, unused devices plugged in.

Turn off monitor, PC, and lights when leaving
Enable power-saving settings on all devices
Unplug chargers when not in use
Advocate for occupancy-sensor lighting

📋 Module 4 · Action Plan

Your Personal Waste Reduction Action Plan

This is the most important exercise in the course — because this is where learning becomes doing. Create a specific, measurable commitment you can act on today.

1 Waste Stream Focus
Close your eyes — picture your office right now. What do you see most of in the bins? That's your waste stream.
2 Your Specific Action This Week
Start with "I will…" — make it specific, doable alone, and visible by Friday. E.g. "I will set my printer default to double-sided printing."
3 How Will You Know You've Succeeded?
Make it binary — either you achieved it or you didn't. E.g. "Zero disposable cups in my bin by Friday."
4 Who Will Support You?
Social accountability doubles your likelihood of following through. Name someone who will check in with you.
5 Review Date
Two weeks is the recommended cycle. Short review loops build lasting habits.
